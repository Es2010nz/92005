"Kaitiaki Zero"

Eliminating predators, restoring balance.

Predator Free New Zealand is a nationwide mission to remove three major introduced predators — rats, stoats, and possums — by 2050. These predators threaten Aotearoa’s unique wildlife, killing millions of native birds, insects, and lizards every year. The goal is simple: restore the natural balance so species like kiwi, kākā, and kererū can thrive again. At the heart of this movement is kaitiakitanga — the Māori principle of guardianship and caring for the environment. Predator Free NZ reflects this value by encouraging communities, iwi, landowners, and volunteers to work together to protect the whenua and the taonga species that live on it. Thousands of people across the country run backyard traps, support local reserves, and help rebuild healthy ecosystems, we at Katiaki Zero want to help with this goal.

Stoat

Stoats are widely regarded as the single most devastating introduced predator to New Zealand’s native wildlife. Originally brought from Europe in the late 1800s to control a rabbit plague, they quickly turned their attention to the country's defenceless endemic birds, which had evolved for millions of years without mammalian predators. Because stoats are incredibly agile climbers, fast runners, and strong swimmers, they can easily access any habitat to raid nests. They are relentless, voracious hunters that engage in "surplus killing"—slaughtering an entire nest of birds or eggs far beyond what they need to eat. Today, stoats are the primary reason why 95% of wild-hatched kiwi chicks die before reaching adulthood, and they have already driven iconic species like the South Island bush wren and the laughing owl to total extinction.

Eradication

To eradicate stoats, New Zealanders are taking aggressive action through the national Predator Free 2050 initiative. Conservation groups and volunteers maintain vast networks of humane kill traps, like the DOC200, using eggs and meat as bait. Experts also deploy scented tracking dogs to find hidden dens and use aerial drops of 1080 poison to clear rugged, inaccessible backcountry. Thanks to these combined efforts, numerous offshore islands are now entirely stoat-free sanctuaries.

Our latest conservation field data highlights clear metrics surrounding local volunteer engagement, regional trap capture frequencies, and the progressive bounceback indices of native wildlife markers across monitoring sanctuaries.
