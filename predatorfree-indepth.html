"Kaitiaki Zero"

Eliminating predators, restoring balance.

Reaping destructive predators (like possums, rats, and stoats) is essential to protect New Zealand’s vulnerable native wildlife from extinction. By actively participating in localized predator control, every Kiwi can embrace kaitiakitanga—the Māori principle of guardianship—to restore the mauri (lifeforce) of our unique landscapes and ensure our native birds thrive for future generations.

Community-led biosecurity initiatives protect regional biodiversity by combining wide-scale backyard trapping with targeted rural firearm hunting to eliminate invasive predators Department of Conservation Cape Sanctuary. Neighbors coordinate through local trap libraries to deploy a multi-layered defense network using specialized equipment tailored to different pest sizes Tukituki Land Care. They manage classic Victor snap traps and automated Goodnature A24 units inside protective boxes for small rats and mice, utilize heavy-duty DOC 150 and 200 steel boxes for medium-sized stoats, and set large live-capture cage traps or Timms traps to catch destructive feral cats and ferrets Hawke's Bay Regional Council Tukituki Land Care. To reinforce these stationary lines, local hunting groups step in with rifles and thermal optics to actively track and shoot large, trap-shy pests across rugged terrain Cape Sanctuary Gisborne District Council. This unified, all-of-community defense leaves no gaps for pests to multiply, creating a massive regional safety shield that allows native birdlife and local ecosystems to successfully recover Tukituki Land Care Predator Free Hawke's Bay.

Others

"Possums" strip forest canopies bare and devour millions of native eggs and chicks annually. "Rats" have wiped out a quarter of native bird species, with climbing ship rats raiding nests. "Wild cats" are apex predators that have driven 14 native bird species to extinction. "Wild dogs" form roaming packs that cause catastrophic damage by killing flightless adult kiwis. "Hedgehogs" are underrated threats that feast heavily on native wētā, rare lizards, and shorebird eggs.

The graph shows that rats and possums are the most common pests found near local homes, leading many residents to trap them. Even though dangerous predators like stoats and ferrets are rarely seen in backyards, volunteers still spend a lot of time trapping them in the wider community. This hands-on help from New Zealanders is critical because these introduced pests kill millions of native birds, lizards, and insects every year. Since our unique wildlife evolved without land mammals, they have no natural defenses. Backyard trapping by everyday citizens creates safe zones that official conservation teams cannot reach alone, saving local species from extinction.
